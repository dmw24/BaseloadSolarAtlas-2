Harnessing the Sun

Can solar and batteries deliver reliable power around the clock? A global exploration.

By the Baseload Solar Atlas Team • January 2026
expand_more Scroll to explore

The Solar Paradox

The sun delivers more energy to Earth in a single hour than humanity consumes in an entire year. Solar panels have become astonishingly cheap—falling 99% in cost over four decades. Yet a persistent challenge remains: the sun does not shine at night, and clouds roll in without regard for electricity demand.

This simple fact has long been the trump card of solar sceptics. Baseload power—the steady, round-the-clock electricity that keeps hospitals humming and factories churning—has traditionally been the domain of coal plants and nuclear reactors. The intermittency of solar, so the argument goes, renders it a mere supplement to "real" power generation.

But this argument is growing stale. Battery storage costs have plummeted 90% since 2010. A new question emerges: where on Earth can solar panels and batteries, working in tandem, provide reliable baseload power—and at what cost?

Mapping the Solar Potential

To answer this question requires a global perspective. Using hourly solar irradiance data from NASA for thousands of locations worldwide, it is possible to simulate how a solar-plus-battery system would perform against a constant 1 megawatt load—the equivalent of powering roughly 800 homes.

The map reveals a gradient of opportunity. The ochre and amber regions—the Sahara, the Arabian Peninsula, Chile's Atacama Desert, and Australia's outback—achieve capacity factors above 90%, meaning the solar-plus-battery system could meet demand more than nine hours out of every ten.

But deserts, however sun-blessed, are far from where people actually live.

The Storage Revolution

Battery storage transforms the equation. With no batteries at all, even the sunniest locations struggle to provide power after dark. But as storage capacity increases, the world's viable solar baseload zones expand dramatically.

At 8 megawatt-hours of storage per megawatt of baseload demand—roughly equivalent to a night's worth of backup—much of the tropics and subtropics turns viable. Push storage to 24 MWh, and reliable solar baseload becomes possible across vast swaths of Southern Europe, the American Southwest, and much of Africa.

The physics is unforgiving at the poles: no amount of storage can compensate for months of winter darkness above 60 degrees latitude. But for the 6.5 billion people living in the tropics and mid-latitudes, the picture looks far more promising than conventional wisdom suggests.

The Cost Equation

Reliability is one thing; affordability is another. The Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) accounts for everything: the capital cost of solar panels and batteries, financing charges, operational expenses, and the inevitable degradation of equipment over decades.

At today's prices—roughly $600 per kilowatt of solar capacity and $120 per kilowatt-hour of battery storage—the economics are surprisingly competitive. Across much of the Global South, solar baseload can be delivered for $60-90 per megawatt-hour, matching or undercutting combined-cycle gas plants in many markets.

The sweet spots are where one might expect: regions with intense, consistent sunlight and relatively low financing costs. But the map also reveals surprises. Parts of East Africa and South Asia, despite their reputation for unreliable grids, could generate solar baseload power more cheaply than Europe or Japan.

Where People Actually Live

A technology's potential matters only if it can reach those who need it. Overlaying population density onto the solar resource map reveals the central tension: the world's best solar resources often lie far from the world's largest energy consumers.

Yet the picture is not as bleak as the deserts suggest. More than 4 billion people live in regions where solar-plus-battery systems could achieve capacity factors above 85%. India, Indonesia, Nigeria, and Brazil—among the world's most populous and fastest-growing economies—all fall comfortably within the solar-viable zone.

The Access Imperative

For nearly a billion people, the question is not whether solar can compete with existing power plants—it is whether any reliable electricity can be had at all. The map of energy access tells a sobering story: across sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia, grid reliability remains woefully low.

Here, solar baseload is not merely competitive—it may be transformative. For communities where the existing grid delivers power less than half the time, a solar-plus-battery system rated at 90% capacity factor would represent a quantum leap in reliability.

Displacing Fossil Fuels

The climate case for solar is clearest where it can directly displace the dirtiest power plants. The world still operates over 2,000 gigawatts of coal-fired capacity, much of it in regions with excellent solar resources.

India, with 230 gigawatts of coal capacity, sits beneath some of the world's most favourable solar irradiance. China's coal heartland in the north faces more challenging conditions, but the country's southern provinces—home to substantial thermal capacity—offer far better solar potential.

The prize is enormous: replacing even a fraction of the world's coal fleet with solar baseload would deliver emissions reductions on a scale no other technology can match.

The Path Forward

The geography of energy is being rewritten. Solar-plus-battery systems can already provide reliable baseload power across much of the inhabited world, at costs competitive with fossil fuels. As battery prices continue their descent—projected to fall another 50% by 2030—the viable zone will expand further still.

The remaining obstacles are not technical but political and financial: permitting delays, grid interconnection queues, and the challenge of mobilising capital at the scale required. Yet the underlying economics are increasingly irresistible.

The sun has always offered humanity its most abundant energy source. Now, for the first time, we have the technology to harness it around the clock.
